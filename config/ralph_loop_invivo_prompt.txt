WHAT "BEATING LS" MEANS ON IN-VIVO DATA:
Since there's no ground truth CBF/ATT, we compare on quality metrics:
  - CoV (coefficient of variation) within GM tissue: LOWER = less noisy = BETTER
  - Spatial smoothness (gradient magnitude within brain): LOWER = smoother = BETTER
  - GM/WM contrast ratio: should be 2.0-3.5 (physiologically plausible)
  - GM CBF mean: should be 30-90 ml/100g/min
  - GM ATT mean: should be 800-1800 ms
The NN's architectural advantage is SPATIAL CONTEXT. It should produce SMOOTHER,
LESS NOISY maps than per-voxel LS fitting. If it doesn't, the training is wrong.

LIKELY ROOT CAUSES TO INVESTIGATE:
1. SIGNAL PREPROCESSING MISMATCH: The in-vivo npy signals may not match training
   signal scale. Check invivo_comparison.py run_nn_on_subject() — it applies
   M0_scale*global_scale = 100*10 = 1000x. Is this correct? Compare actual in-vivo
   brain signal ranges to what the model sees during training.

2. TRAINING DATA DISTRIBUTION: SpatialPhantomGenerator creates synthetic phantoms
   with geometric tissue regions. Real brains look NOTHING like this. The model may
   be learning phantom-specific patterns that don't transfer.

3. NOISE MODEL MISMATCH: Training uses Gaussian noise. In-vivo has Rician noise,
   physiological noise, motion artifacts, B0 inhomogeneity. The model may be
   learning to denoise the wrong kind of noise.

4. DOMAIN RANDOMIZATION RANGES: The alpha_BS1, T1_artery, labeling efficiency
   ranges may not cover the actual patient variability in this dataset.

5. NORMALIZATION CHAIN: The z-score denormalization (pred * std + mean) uses
   norm_stats from TRAINING data. If training distribution doesn't match in-vivo,
   the denormalized values will be systematically biased.
